# NHS A&E Batch Dashboard — Presentation Script (Redesigned Gold Layer)

---

## Opening — set the scene before touching the screen

*(Stand confidently. Don't click anything yet. Make eye contact first.)*

"Every month, over 1 million people in England walk into an Accident and Emergency department.
The NHS has one clear promise to every single one of them: be seen, assessed, and treated within
4 hours. It's not just a target — it's the standard the entire system is measured against.

This dashboard is built on 3 years of real NHS England data — April 2023 to March 2026 — covering
215 hospital trusts across every region of England. What I'm going to show you in the next few
minutes isn't a summary of that data. It's the story inside it."

*(Now open the dashboard on screen — Page 1.)*

---

## Page 1 — The Executive View

### KPI Cards (top row)

"Let's start with the headline numbers. These five cards give you the full picture in under
10 seconds."

- **"33 million."** That's how many A&E attendances happened across England in this 3-year period.
  Not visits to a GP, not 999 calls — actual people walking through an A&E door. That's
  roughly half the population of England, every single year.

- **"215 trusts tracked."** This isn't a sample — it covers every NHS organisation reporting
  A&E data to NHS England. Every major hospital, every region.

- **"26.7%."** *(Pause here. Let it land.)* More than 1 in 4 patients waited longer than
  4 hours. The NHS target is that fewer than 5% should wait that long. We are sitting at
  more than 5 times that level. This number alone tells you there is a systemic problem, not
  an isolated one.

- **"0.09%."** This is the average improvement in 4-hour performance across all trusts,
  period over period. Not 9%. Not even 1%. Less than a tenth of a percent. The system is
  essentially static — it is neither recovering nor getting dramatically worse. It is stuck.

- **"1 million."** *(Point to the red card — deliberately alarming.)* One million patients
  waited more than 12 hours in A&E across this 3-year period. Not 4 hours — 12 hours. Half
  a day, sitting in an emergency department. This isn't in the headline breach statistics most
  people quote — it's hidden inside the data. It shouldn't be."

---

### Trend Chart — "National A&E Performance & 12hr Waits Trend"

"Now look at this chart. There are two lines here — and together they tell a story that one
line alone couldn't."

*(Point to each line.)*

"The **blue line** is the percentage of patients seen within 4 hours — national performance.
The **red dashed line at the top** is the 95% NHS target. The **red solid line** is the number
of patients waiting over 12 hours each month."

*(Trace both lines slowly.)*

"Four things jump out:

**First** — the gap. The blue performance line has never once touched the 95% target in this
entire 3-year period. Not a single month. The best the country managed was around 77-78%.

**Second** — the winter dips. Every January, the blue line drops. A&E is always harder in
winter. What's concerning is how far below the target even the summer months sit — there's
no genuine recovery, just a slight bounce before the next winter.

**Third — and this is the alarming one** — look at the red solid line. 12-hour waits are not
flat. They are climbing. That spike you can see toward early 2026 is nearly 80,000 patients
waiting over 12 hours in a single month. That number is getting worse, not better, even as
the 4-hour performance line stays flat.

**Fourth** — the date slicer on the right. I can narrow both lines down to any time window
you want — a specific winter, a particular year, the most recent 6 months.
*(Demonstrate by sliding the date range.)*"

---

### Trust Performance Chart — "Trust 4-Hour Performance (Bottom 10)"

"Now this is where it gets specific — and honest. These are the 10 hospital trusts with the
worst 4-hour performance in England over this period. I've applied a minimum attendance
threshold so you're only seeing major A&E departments, not small walk-in centres."

*(Point to the RAG legend on the right.)*

"The colour coding follows the same RAG standard NHS uses in its own performance reports:
Green means the trust is hitting the 95% target. Amber means they're between 70 and 95%.
Red means below 70% — significantly underperforming."

*(Sweep your cursor across the all-red bars.)*

"Every single bar here is red. Every one of these trusts is seeing fewer than 70% of their
patients within 4 hours. Some are down at 45-50%. That means more than half of all patients
at these hospitals are waiting longer than the NHS promises they should.

Mid Cheshire, University Hospitals, Royal United — these aren't small local units. These are
major regional hospitals serving hundreds of thousands of people.

This chart exists so that conversation stops being abstract. '26.7% breach rate nationally'
sounds like a statistic. Seeing it broken down trust by trust, in red, makes it a real
operational problem with real names attached."

### Regional Chart — "Regional A&E Volume vs Performance"

"The bars show total A&E attendances per region — volume. The line shows average 4-hour
performance — quality. Together they answer: which regions are under the most pressure, and
are they performing proportionally?"

*(Point to the Midlands bar — the tallest.)*

"NHS England Midlands has the highest volume — nearly 30 million attendances across this period.
But look at where the performance line sits for that region — not at the top. High volume and
lower performance tend to go together, and you can see that pattern play out across the chart.

London has very high attendances — it's the most densely populated region — but its performance
line fluctuates considerably, reflecting how differently individual London trusts perform.

The practical takeaway for a business or commissioning audience: if you're deciding where to
focus investment, staffing support, or pathway improvement programmes, this regional view is
your starting point. It's the difference between spreading resources evenly and targeting them
where the pressure is genuinely greatest."

---

### Demographic Chart — "A&E Attendance by Demographics"

"Finally, this chart answers the question every planner and commissioner needs to know: who
is actually using A&E?"

*(The slicer on the right shows breakdown type — currently showing 'age'.)*

"Starting with age — and this finding is important enough to highlight directly: the two
largest bars represent working-age adults, not the elderly. The common assumption is that
A&E demand is driven primarily by older patients. The data shows a more complex picture —
adults between 25 and 64 are the largest group attending A&E in England. That has significant
implications for when services are most stretched, what conditions are presenting, and how
staffing models should be designed.

*(Click 'gender' in the slicer.)*

I can switch to gender breakdown — showing the split between male and female attendances.

*(Click 'chief_complaint'.)*

Or chief complaint — what people are actually coming in for. This tells you where clinical
pathways need to be strongest, and where preventable A&E visits might be reduced with better
community provision upstream.

*(Click 'ethnicity'.)*

And ethnicity — which is important for understanding whether services are equitably accessible
and whether specific communities face different barriers or experiences."

### Trust Improvement Chart — "Trust Performance — Biggest Improvers (Top 10)"

"This chart is new — and it's the most forward-looking thing on this dashboard. It shows the
10 trusts that have improved their 4-hour performance the most, period over period."

*(Point to the green bars.)*

"Nems UTC, Wrightington Wigan, Kingston and Richmond — these trusts have genuinely moved.
The green bars show positive period-over-period improvement. The amber bars show trusts that
are flat but not declining.

Why does this matter? Because it answers the question the bottom 10 chart on Page 1 can't:
what does recovery actually look like, and where is it already happening? These trusts are
proof that improvement is possible. They're the case studies for what intervention and
operational change can achieve.

If I were advising on where to focus knowledge-sharing programmes or peer-support between
trusts, I would start with these names — find out what they did differently, and replicate it
in the trusts still stuck in red."

## Recommendations — what the data suggests doing

"Based on what this data shows, three things stand out as immediate priorities:

**1. Target the bottom 10 trusts with system support — and learn from the improvers.**
These trusts have been in the red for 3 years. Monitoring alone hasn't moved the needle —
the 0.09% average improvement proves that. But the Trust Improvement chart on Page 2 shows
recovery IS possible — some trusts have meaningfully improved. The priority should be pairing
struggling trusts with high-improvers for structured peer learning, not just issuing
performance notices.

**2. Plan around working-age demand, not just winter surge.**
If the assumption in workforce planning is that A&E is primarily an elderly-patient problem,
this data challenges that directly. Staffing models and community alternatives need to be
calibrated to the actual demographic mix, not the assumed one.

**3. Act on the 12-hour wait trend before it becomes the headline.**
The 4-hour breach rate is the number everyone quotes. But the 12-hour wait trend on Page 1
is climbing — nearly 80,000 patients in a single month by early 2026. Once that number becomes
the story in the press, it will be too late to get ahead of it. The data is already showing
where this is heading.

**4. Use the regional volume data to prioritise capital and workforce investment.**
NHS England Midlands and London carry the highest volumes. If the national system is going
to recover toward the 95% target — even partially — those regions need proportionally greater
resource than a uniform national approach would provide."

## The Ask — end with something concrete

*(Put down the clicker. Speak directly.)*

"The 95% target hasn't been met in 3 years. The trend line shows a system that has flatlined,
not recovered. What I'd like from this group is a decision on which of these three priorities
to move on first — so that the next time we look at this dashboard, the story inside the data
has started to change."

## If Asked: Limitations to acknowledge

"To be transparent about what this data can and can't tell us:

- This analysis is based on NHS England's published statistical data — which is excellent for
  trend analysis but doesn't capture why individual trusts are underperforming. Causation
  needs operational investigation alongside this data.
- The Gold layer was rebuilt with 8 aggregated tables, replacing the original 11. This
  introduces some discontinuity with prior reporting if you're comparing to older versions
  of this dashboard.
- The 'Avg Trust Improvement' metric (0.09%) represents an average of period-over-period
  changes across all trusts — trusts that are improving cancel out those that are declining,
  so the aggregate can mask significant individual variation in both directions."
